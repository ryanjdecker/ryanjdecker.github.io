Ethics and morality

Is there a difference between ethics and morality?

There's a discussion of this in the entry on Modern Morality and Ancient Ethics in the Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy.

The Greek ēthikós was the adjectival form of êthos, "character; custom".

The Roman politician and philosopher Cicero translated this as moralis, the adjectival form of Latin mos, "custom."

The Greek word was later borrowed into Latin more directly as ethica, and then both terms came into English through Old French.

According to the entry, different philosophers have distinguished between these terms in different ways.

I suspect that the important difference in usage is that ethics is used more in academic and professional contexts while morality is more common.

Because of that, it seems like ethics is more associated with theory while morality is more associated with the subject of ethical theory (what the IEP entry describes as the standard position).

The IEP entry also notes that Peter Singer treats the views synonymously.

As for me, I'm going to follow Peter Singer's approach and treat the terms synonymously.

I think that finer-grained distinctions, between theory and subject or between character and custom and so on, can be made in the situations where they are important, rather than trying to load them into the modern words ethics and morality.

In general, i think that debates about the "correct" meanings of closely related terms are often a fruitless distraction.
